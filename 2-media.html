Media


Watercolor


Watercolor is made by combining finely ground pigment in a water-soluble binder that distributes pigment particles to create a uniform solution and adheres it to the support. It is most commonly applied with a brush and water to paper, which is typically white. The brightness of the underlying support contributes to the jewel-like quality of the transparent colors. Watercolor paper is usually treated with gelatin sizing to prevent the paint from penetrating the sheet and allows for more precise brushwork.

I first started taking watercolor classes at a local university's extension program in 2007. Over the years, I learned to work with this medium, which is both forgiving and unforgiving at the same time. It is a transparent medium that can be layered to achieve luminosity and increased color saturation. Various effects can be achieved depending on whether the paper is wet or dry, how much water has been added to the paint, and the type of brush with which the watercolor paint is applied.

I use the Winsor & Newton Professional Watercolour range for its brilliance, transparency, and lightfastness. I paint on Arches 100% cotton cold-pressed watercolor paper for its texture and resistance to repeated working and layering. This paper is mould-made and gelatin-sized to prevent paint from penetrating into the paper and to improve the paper's workability. My favorite watercolor brushes to paint with are Robert Simmons White Sable synthetic brushes, which have a nice spring and keep a fine point when wet.

My Favorite Watercolor Supplies


  • Paint: Winsor & Newton Professional Watercolor tubes
  • Paper: Arches 140 lb cold-pressed 100% cotton watercolor paper
  • Brushes: Robert Simmons White Sapphire Series synthetic brushes



Pastel


Soft pastels are composed of kaolin, a type of clay, finely ground pigment, and a very small amount of binder. These ingredients are combined and rolled into sticks of colored powder that have to be hard enough to hold their shape, yet soft enough to crumble when stroked across a page. The surface to which it is applied must have a slight texture, or "tooth", to grab and hold the pastel on the surface. Soft pastels are available in soft pastel sticks, in pans that can be applied with a sponge-like tool, and as harder pastel pencils.

A pastel painting's optical vibrancy is the result of both the absence of yellowing resins in its composition and the reflection of light from the multiple facets of the finely ground powder. Unlike watercolor, it is an opaque medium that does not depend on the underlying brightness of the paper to impart luminosity.

This medium is the one I have come to learn most recently, and despite the relatively short amount of time I have been working with them, I have to say they are my favorite media to work in. The vibrancy of color, ability to layer, and the tactile experience of working with them when blending has made them my most enjoyable medium.

I use Rembrandt soft pastels for the majority of my work. They are a medium-hard pastel that are hard enough to create sharp details yet soft enough to blend easily. Unison pastels are very soft, and I often use them over top of Rembrandt pastels to create highlights and accents. For the finest details, I use Faber-Castell Pitt pastel pencils. Although there are many pastel surfaces available, my favorite is Pastelmat. This cellulose support has a velvet-like texture that is less abrasive than sanded paper, yet has enough tooth to accept multiple layers of pastel.


My Favorite Pastel Supplies


  • Soft Pastel Sticks: Rembrandt and Unison
  • Pastel Pencils: Faber-Castell Pitt Pastel Pencils
  • Paper: Clairefontaine Pastelmat
